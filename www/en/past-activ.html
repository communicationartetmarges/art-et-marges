Introduction to Art Brut. Invented by French painter Jean Dubuffet in the mid-twentieth century, this concept first applied to the collection he built up from the works of psychiatric hospital patients, identifying in them a form of authenticity and necessity he couldn't find in what he called “cultural” art. Today, Art Brut more broadly encompasses creations born outside the established world of contemporary art, bearing witness to a strong artistic personality.

Look forward to a panorama that unfolds before your eyes a few emblematic artists of this art “that doesn't lie down in the beds we've made for it”.

The tour will explore the artist's favorite themes: the arts, science and mathematics.
Michel Goyon explores all the paths of knowledge surrounding the mystery of life, from the history of the earth and the universe, to physics and astronomy, anatomy and the complex structure of living bodies, all sprinkled with popular imagery, contemporary art and art brut.
His experiments gradually led to a passion for the relationship between figure, writing and calculation, and he discovered a new language for apprehending reality: mathematics. Arithmetic, algebra and geometry became his three primary colors.
He enthusiastically explored systems, sequences and arborescences, attempting to reveal the profound links between the microscopic and the macroscopic, numbers and shapes, the interior of bodies and galaxies.

We'll also look at his specific artistic techniques:

  • Image transfer. Artisanal techniques using animal fat, glue and rubbing, or DIY stencils.
  • Cut-out pages. The artist develops the almost infinite possibilities of cutting and folding.
  • Bookbinding. The artist takes advantage of different types of binding to invent hybrid objects in the form of robots, incorporating folds, cut-outs, phosphorus paint and the assembly of recycled objects.

"On February 18, 1976, La Devinière, a place of institutional psychotherapy, opened its doors to 19 children deemed incurable and rejected by all. Neither common sense, nor psychiatry, nor pedagogy could admit or recognize them. These children, in short exiled, La Devinière accepted them definitively with the founding principle of not rejecting them under any pretext. The word "asylum" takes on its true meaning, a space without grids or chemicals, where one is given the right to "live with one's madness". For more than twenty years, bonds of solidarity have been forged between those who had nothing to link them. In the late 1980s and early 1990s, Ariane Bergrichter (1937-1996) created countless drawings in a Brussels teeming with cafés, people at tables, bus stops, passers-by and artists, ladies in heavy coats, waiters and workers.

Twenty years after the artist's death, her daughter discovers her mother's work, each composition neatly folded and stored in a suitcase whose treasures she did not suspect.
